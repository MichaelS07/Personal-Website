Google May 2026 Core Update: What's Changing and What to Do This Week

May 22, 2026 • SEO•7 min read

Google's May 2026 core update began rolling out this week — officially confirmed, globally scoped, and expected to take approximately two weeks to fully settle. This is the second core update of 2026, following March's broad quality refresh, and early signals suggest it is more targeted than its predecessor. If your clients' organic traffic is moving right now, this update is almost certainly the cause.

The focus this time is sharper than the typical "helpful content" framing Google uses in public communications. Search practitioners watching the early volatility data and Google's own documentation are seeing a consistent thread: the update appears to be separating content authored by genuine experts with real-world experience from content that is mass-produced using AI generation tools. That distinction is increasingly the fault line along which Google is reorganising its rankings — and it has direct implications for how you approach content strategy across every client vertical.

Here is what the update actually targets, how to diagnose whether your clients are affected, and the actions that are most likely to produce a measurable response — not in six months, but in the next two to four weeks.

What Google Is Actually Targeting This Time

Google has not published detailed signals for the May 2026 update — it never does. But between the rollout behaviour, early ranking volatility patterns, and the direction of travel from Google's Search Quality Evaluator Guidelines, the picture is reasonably clear for practitioners who have been tracking this closely.

The update is intensifying Google's ability to distinguish between two categories of content that often look superficially similar: content written by someone who has actually done the thing they are describing, and content that aggregates and synthesises information from existing sources — even competently — without the author having direct experience of the subject matter. The former is what Google now consistently calls "experience" in its E-E-A-T framework (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness). The latter, regardless of how well-written it is, is increasingly at risk.

This does not mean AI-assisted content is categorically penalised. Google has been explicit that AI generation is not itself a ranking factor. What matters is whether the content demonstrates the signals of genuine expertise — specificity, first-hand perspective, original analysis, demonstrable authorship — regardless of the tools used to produce it. The practical implication: generic, templated content that covers broad topics with surface-level accuracy is the category under pressure. Niche content with genuine depth, clear authorship, and original insight is being rewarded.

User Intent Matching Is the Other Lever Being Pulled

Alongside the content quality signals, this update is continuing Google's multi-year shift away from keyword matching toward intent satisfaction as the primary ranking determinant. The question Google is increasingly asking of any piece of content is not "does this page contain the query keyword?" but "does this page actually satisfy what the person asking this query needed?"

In practice, this means pages that rank for broad informational terms but have high bounce rates, low dwell time, and low scroll depth are being evaluated differently than before. User experience signals — not just as defined by Core Web Vitals, but as inferred from engagement behaviour — are now weighted more heavily in how Google assesses whether a page is genuinely satisfying the intent behind a search. If your content answers the first question a user might have but fails to address the follow-up questions that define the full intent, Google is increasingly able to detect that gap.

This is particularly important for the informational and commercial investigation content categories — the "what is", "how to", and "best X for Y" query types that form the bulk of most content marketing strategies. These are precisely the query types where the gap between surface-level coverage and genuine intent satisfaction is widest, and where the May 2026 update appears to be drawing the sharpest distinctions.

💡 Pro Tip

Pull your top-20 organic landing pages by traffic in Google Search Console. For each one, ask: does this page answer not just the exact query, but the three most likely follow-up questions? If you would not recommend this page to a friend asking the question, Google's quality systems are increasingly able to form the same judgement. Cross-reference with GA4 scroll depth and session duration to identify which pages are underperforming on engagement signals relative to their ranking position.

How to Diagnose Whether Your Clients Are Affected

Core update impact takes time to fully surface — the May 2026 update will be rolling for approximately two weeks from its announcement date, meaning some of what you see in the next few days reflects early volatility rather than settled signal. That said, the diagnostic process should start now.

The first step is to segment your Google Search Console data by landing page and query type. Look for pages that have moved materially — more than 20% — in average position over the last seven days. Separate this movement by query intent: informational queries will typically be the most volatile; transactional and navigational queries tend to be more stable during core updates. If you are seeing informational content drop while transactional pages hold, that is consistent with what this update appears to be targeting.

The second diagnostic step is authorship and E-E-A-T audit. For the pages that have dropped, assess whether they have a clearly identified author with demonstrable expertise, whether the content cites original sources or first-hand experience, and whether the topic depth goes beyond what could be produced by aggregating the top-ranking results on that topic. Pages that score poorly on these dimensions and have seen ranking drops are most likely affected by the May update, not by an unrelated technical or link factor.

Third — and this is important — separate genuine update impact from volatility. Core updates generate noise. Not every ranking movement in the next two weeks is a signal from this update. Focus your diagnostic energy on pages that moved more than 20% in average position and that have meaningful traffic at stake. Small fluctuations on low-traffic pages are not worth actioning until the update has fully settled.

The Actions That Actually Move the Needle

Google's standard guidance after any core update is to focus on content quality — which is accurate, but not particularly useful at the operational level. Here is what "content quality" actually means in terms of specific actions for affected clients right now.

Add demonstrable author expertise to affected pages. This means real author profiles with credentials, social proof, and relevant professional history — not a byline with a first name. If a page covers a specialist topic, the author attribution should reflect someone with genuine expertise in that area. This is a ranking factor that Google can evaluate through structured data, linked author profiles, and the body of content attributed to an author across the web.

Deepen content with original analysis and first-hand specificity. For the pages that have dropped, identify where the content is generic — statements that are true but could apply to any article on the topic — and replace them with specific, original observations. First-hand case examples, proprietary data, or direct quotes from subject matter experts all add the kind of specificity that distinguishes genuine expertise from aggregated coverage.

Restructure content around full intent, not just the primary query. Map the three to five follow-up questions a user is likely to ask after their initial query and ensure the content addresses them in order of relevance. This is both an E-E-A-T signal and a user experience signal — it reduces the likelihood of a user returning to the SERP to find the answer elsewhere, which Google interprets as a satisfaction failure.

Do not rush to make changes during the rollout. This is the most counterintuitive piece of advice, but it matters. Core update recoveries are evaluated by Google over weeks and months, not days. Making sweeping content changes during a live rollout can introduce additional volatility. Diagnose now, plan your content improvements, and begin implementing systematically once the update has settled — typically two to three weeks after the official announcement date.

The Broader Implication for Content Strategy in 2026

The May 2026 core update is not an isolated event — it is the latest step in a direction Google has been moving consistently for three years. The pattern is clear: content produced at volume without genuine expertise is being systematically displaced in favour of content that demonstrates direct experience, specific knowledge, and authentic authorship. The agencies and in-house teams that recognise this early enough to restructure their content approach will have a material competitive advantage over those still operating on 2022-era content strategy assumptions.

What this means practically is that the content investment calculus is shifting. Producing fifty average articles a month is increasingly a worse use of budget than producing ten exceptional ones — where exceptional means genuinely expert, structurally complete, and demonstrably more useful than anything else ranking for the target query. The clients who will win in organic search over the next twelve months are those whose content reflects actual expertise, not those who have produced the most content.

If your current content production model is optimised for volume and keyword coverage, the May 2026 core update is the clearest signal yet that the model needs to change. The good news is that the path forward is genuinely straightforward — better content, clearly attributed, built for real intent satisfaction. The challenge is execution at the account management level, which is where the real work happens.
